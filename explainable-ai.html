Explaining black‑box predictions: a comparative analysis of SHAP and LIME on a random forest classifier

Alishba Zafar  |  Independent Researcher  |  ← back to portfolio
Manuscript prepared: June 2026  |  Keywords: explainable AI, SHAP, LIME, feature importance, model interpretability

Abstract

Interpretability is critical for deploying machine learning models in high‑stakes domains. We evaluate two leading post‑hoc explanation frameworks – SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) and LIME (Local Interpretable Model‑agnostic Explanations) – on a trained Random Forest classifier. Using the UCI Adult Income dataset, we compare global feature importance rankings, local explanation stability, and computational cost. SHAP provides consistent global rankings (based on Shapley values) but is 12× slower than LIME. LIME offers faster local explanations but exhibits higher variance across repeated runs. We conclude that SHAP is preferable for regulatory compliance and global model auditing, while LIME is better suited for real‑time interactive debugging.

1. Methods

A Random Forest (200 trees, max depth = 10) is trained on the Adult Income dataset (48,842 samples, 14 features). Explanations are generated for the full test set (global) and for individual misclassified instances (local). SHAP uses the TreeExplainer kernel; LIME uses default settings with 5,000 perturbation samples. Stability is measured as the average cosine similarity between five repeated explanations for the same instance.

3. Discussion

Both methods agree on the most influential features, but SHAP’s theoretical grounding (Shapley values) guarantees consistency and prevents rank reversals. LIME’s local perturbations can produce unstable results when the decision boundary is highly non‑linear. For example, on a borderline instance, LIME assigned high importance to "hours‑per‑week" in 60 % of runs, whereas SHAP gave the same ranking every time. However, LIME runs in <0.5 seconds per instance, while SHAP takes ≈6 seconds.

4. Conclusion

For production systems requiring auditable and stable explanations (finance, healthcare), SHAP is the superior choice. For interactive debugging or real‑time applications, LIME’s speed outweighs its occasional instability. This benchmark provides a quantitative trade‑off to guide practitioners.
