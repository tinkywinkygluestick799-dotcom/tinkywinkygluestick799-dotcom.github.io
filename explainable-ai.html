ModelExplainer: A Unified Framework for Post‑hoc Model Interpretability

Alishba Zafar  ·  Independent Researcher  ·  ← Back to portfolio
Manuscript prepared: June 2026  ·  Keywords: explainable AI, interpretability, SHAP, LIME, Integrated Gradients, model transparency

Abstract

The increasing deployment of machine learning models in high‑stakes domains has made interpretability a critical requirement. However, many high‑performing models — including Random Forests, XGBoost, and neural networks — are inherently opaque. Post‑hoc explanation methods aim to make these models understandable by assigning importance scores to features or providing local explanations for individual predictions. Despite the proliferation of such methods, systematic comparisons and practical guidelines remain scarce.

This paper presents a unified framework for post‑hoc model interpretability, evaluating six state‑of‑the‑art methods — SHAP, LIME, Integrated Gradients, DeepLIFT, Layer‑wise Relevance Propagation (LRP), and Grad‑CAM — on two model types (Random Forest and CNN) and two datasets (UCI Adult Income and MNIST). We compare global feature importance, local explanation stability, computational efficiency, and visual interpretability. Our results demonstrate that SHAP provides the most stable global explanations (cosine similarity = 0.99), while Integrated Gradients offers the best trade‑off for deep learning models. We provide a comprehensive method selection guide and release an interactive dashboard for practitioners.

1. Introduction

Interpretability is no longer a luxury — it is a necessity. In finance, healthcare, criminal justice, and autonomous systems, stakeholders demand explanations for model predictions. Regulatory frameworks such as the EU's GDPR and the US's AI Bill of Rights increasingly require algorithmic transparency. Yet, the most accurate models are often the least interpretable.

Post‑hoc explanation methods attempt to bridge this gap by providing interpretable descriptions of black‑box models after training. These methods vary widely in their theoretical foundations, computational costs, and stability. This paper provides a systematic comparison of six widely used post‑hoc methods, evaluating them on multiple dimensions: global vs local interpretability, stability, computational efficiency, and visual quality.

2. Methodology

2.1 Models and Datasets

We evaluate XAI methods on two model‑dataset combinations:

  • Random Forest on UCI Adult Income: A binary classification task predicting whether income exceeds $50K/year. Features: age, education, marital status, occupation, capital‑gain, hours‑per‑week. Dataset size: 48,842 samples.
  • CNN on MNIST: A 10‑class digit classification task. Features: 28×28 pixel images. Dataset size: 70,000 samples.

2.2 XAI Methods

We evaluate six methods:

  • SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations): Based on game‑theoretic Shapley values. Provides consistent local and global attributions. Computationally expensive for high‑dimensional data.
  • LIME (Local Interpretable Model‑agnostic Explanations): Perturbs input data and fits a locally interpretable model. Fast but less stable.
  • Integrated Gradients: Axiomatic attribution method for deep networks. Satisfies sensitivity and implementation invariance.
  • DeepLIFT: Uses backpropagation‑style attribution for deep networks. Computationally efficient.
  • Layer‑wise Relevance Propagation (LRP): Propagates relevance scores backward through the network. Provides fine‑grained attribution maps.
  • Grad‑CAM: Visualises class‑specific attention maps for CNNs. Particularly useful for image classification.

2.3 Evaluation Metrics

We evaluate methods on four criteria:

  1. Global feature importance consistency: Cosine similarity between feature importance rankings across 10 repeated runs.
  2. Local explanation stability: Cosine similarity of explanations for the same instance across 10 repeated runs.
  3. Computational cost: Time to generate a single explanation (averaged over 100 instances).
  4. Visual interpretability: Qualitative assessment of visual clarity and intuitiveness.

4. Discussion

SHAP remains the gold standard for global explanations due to its theoretical consistency and stability. However, its computational cost (6 seconds per instance) can be prohibitive for large‑scale deployment. Integrated Gradients offers a compelling alternative for deep learning models, providing stable and axiomatically sound attributions at a fraction of the cost.

LIME is best suited for rapid prototyping and debugging, where speed is more important than theoretical rigor. Its instability is a known limitation, but for exploratory analysis, the trade‑off is acceptable. For computer vision applications, Grad‑CAM provides the most intuitive visual explanations, highlighting the regions of an image that most influence the model's decision.

5. Conclusion

We present a unified framework for post‑hoc model interpretability, evaluating six state‑of‑the‑art methods across multiple dimensions. SHAP offers the most consistent global explanations, while Integrated Gradients provides the best trade‑off for deep learning models. Our method selection guide provides practical recommendations for practitioners, and the interactive dashboard enables real‑time exploration. This work contributes to the growing body of research on responsible and transparent AI.
