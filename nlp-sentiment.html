TextSense: A Comprehensive Benchmark of Transformer Architectures for Multi-Domain Sentiment Analysis

Alishba Zafar  ·  Independent Researcher  ·  ← Back to portfolio
Manuscript prepared: June 2026  ·  Keywords: natural language processing, sentiment analysis, transformers, BERT, RoBERTa, topic modeling

Abstract

Sentiment analysis is a fundamental natural language processing task with broad applications across domains. The emergence of transformer‑based models has significantly advanced the state of the art, yet systematic comparisons across multiple architectures and datasets remain sparse. This paper presents a comprehensive benchmark of six transformer models — BERT, RoBERTa, XLNet, ALBERT, DistilBERT, and ELECTRA — on sentiment classification across three distinct domains: IMDb movie reviews, Twitter social media posts, and Amazon product reviews.

Our results demonstrate that RoBERTa achieves the highest overall accuracy (93.4% on IMDb, 87.1% on Twitter, 91.8% on Amazon), outperforming BERT by 0.9–1.7 percentage points across all datasets. We further analyse the trade‑offs between model size, inference speed, and performance, and provide practical guidelines for model selection. Additionally, we apply LDA topic modeling to uncover latent thematic structures in reviews, offering interpretable insights beyond classification accuracy.

1. Introduction

Sentiment analysis — the task of automatically determining the emotional tone of a text — has become a cornerstone of modern NLP. Its applications range from social media monitoring and market research to customer feedback analysis and political opinion tracking. While early approaches relied on hand‑crafted features and shallow classifiers, the advent of deep learning — particularly transformer‑based pre‑trained language models — has dramatically improved performance.

However, the proliferation of transformer architectures presents a challenge: practitioners must choose among a growing number of models, each with different pre‑training objectives, data scales, and architectural innovations. This paper aims to provide a systematic, reproducible comparison of six widely used transformer models on three diverse sentiment analysis datasets, covering movie reviews, social media, and e‑commerce.

2. Methodology

2.1 Datasets

We evaluate sentiment classification across three domains:

  • IMDb Reviews: 50,000 movie reviews (25,000 positive, 25,000 negative). Balanced split: 25k training / 25k test.
  • Twitter Sentiment: 20,000 tweets (10,000 positive, 10,000 negative). Split: 16k training / 4k test.
  • Amazon Reviews: 50,000 product reviews (25,000 positive, 25,000 negative). Balanced split: 40k training / 10k test.

2.2 Model Architectures

We evaluate six transformer‑based models:

  • BERT base: 110M parameters, pre‑trained on BooksCorpus + Wikipedia.
  • RoBERTa: 125M parameters, pre‑trained on 160GB text (10× BERT data).
  • XLNet: 110M parameters, permutation‑based autoregressive pre‑training.
  • ALBERT: 12M parameters, parameter‑efficient with factorised embeddings.
  • DistilBERT: 66M parameters, distilled from BERT (40% smaller, 60% faster).
  • ELECTRA: 110M parameters, replaced‑token detection pre‑training.

2.3 Fine‑Tuning Protocol

All models are fine‑tuned using the Hugging Face Transformers library with the following hyperparameters:

  • Learning rate: 2e‑5
  • Batch size: 16
  • Epochs: 3
  • Warm‑up: 10% of total steps
  • Optimiser: AdamW with weight decay

All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU (16 GB VRAM).

3.2 Analysis of RoBERTa's Superiority

RoBERTa's improved performance can be attributed to three key factors:

  1. Larger pre‑training data: RoBERTa is trained on 160GB of text (10× BERT's BooksCorpus + Wikipedia), providing richer and more diverse linguistic knowledge.
  2. Dynamic masking: Unlike BERT's static masking, RoBERTa uses dynamic masking patterns, which improves generalisation.
  3. Longer training: RoBERTa is pre‑trained for more steps with larger batches, resulting in more robust representations.

3.3 Topic Modeling with LDA

We apply Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to the IMDb training set to discover latent thematic structures. Five coherent topics emerged:

  • Topic 1 — Plot & Story: story, plot, twist, ending, character
  • Topic 2 — Acting & Performance: actor, performance, role, cast,演技
  • Topic 3 — Direction & Visuals: director, scene, visual, camera, cinematography
  • Topic 4 — Dialogue & Script: dialogue, script, writing, line, conversation
  • Topic 5 — Sound & Music: music, sound, score, soundtrack, audio

These topics align well with established categories in film criticism, validating the interpretability of LDA for domain‑specific text analysis.

3.4 Error Analysis

We analyse misclassifications across all models and identify three recurring failure modes:

  • Sarcasm detection: "This movie was so good it made me want to die" — 34% error rate.
  • Negation handling: "Not bad, but not great either" — 22% error rate.
  • Mixed sentiment: "Great acting, terrible plot" — 18% error rate.

These findings highlight the need for more robust reasoning in sentiment analysis models, particularly in handling figurative language and compositional sentiment.

4. Discussion

Our results confirm that RoBERTa is the best‑performing transformer for sentiment analysis across domains. The additional pre‑training data and dynamic masking provide a clear advantage over BERT and other architectures. However, the performance gap varies by domain: the gap is largest on Twitter (1.7%), where informal language and domain‑specific vocabulary benefit most from diverse pre‑training data.

DistilBERT offers a compelling trade‑off for production deployment: 91.2% accuracy on IMDb with 60% faster inference and 40% smaller model size. For resource‑constrained environments, DistilBERT is the recommended choice. ALBERT, while parameter‑efficient, underperforms on sentiment tasks, suggesting that its parameter‑sharing strategy compromises representational capacity.

5. Conclusion

We present a comprehensive benchmark of six transformer architectures for sentiment analysis across three domains. RoBERTa achieves state‑of‑the‑art results, while DistilBERT offers the best trade‑off for production deployment. Our error analysis identifies sarcasm and negation as persistent challenges, guiding future research directions. The topic modeling results provide interpretable insights into the thematic structure of movie reviews, complementing classification performance.
