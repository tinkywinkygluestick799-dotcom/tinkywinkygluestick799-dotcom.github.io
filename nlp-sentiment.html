TextSense: Comparing Recurrent and Transformer Architectures for Sentiment Analysis — LSTM versus BERT on the IMDb Movie Review Dataset

Alishba Zafar  ·  Independent Researcher  ·  ← Back to portfolio
Manuscript prepared: June 2026  ·  Keywords: LSTM, BERT, sentiment analysis, IMDb, NLP benchmarking

Abstract

Sentiment analysis is a fundamental task in natural language processing (NLP) with applications in social media monitoring, market research, and customer feedback. Two distinct neural architectures have emerged as leading approaches: recurrent neural networks (specifically, Long Short‑Term Memory, LSTM) and transformer‑based models (specifically, BERT — Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers). This paper provides a quantitative comparison of these two paradigms on the balanced IMDb movie review dataset (50,000 reviews).

We evaluate accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score for a bidirectional LSTM, DistilBERT, and BERT base. Our results show that BERT achieves 92.1 % accuracy, outperforming the LSTM by 4.8 percentage points (87.3 %). The advantage is consistent across all metrics, confirming that transformer‑based pre‑training provides a substantial boost even for relatively small‑scale sentiment tasks. However, the LSTM trains 5× faster and requires significantly less GPU memory (4 GB vs 16 GB), making it a reasonable choice for resource‑constrained environments. DistilBERT offers a middle ground: 91.2 % accuracy at half the training time of BERT. We conclude with practical recommendations for model selection based on computational resources and performance requirements.

1. Introduction

Sentiment analysis is the task of determining the emotional tone behind a piece of text. It has broad applications: companies use it to monitor brand perception, political analysts use it to gauge public opinion, and researchers use it to study social phenomena. The rise of deep learning has significantly improved sentiment analysis accuracy, but the choice of architecture — recurrent or transformer — remains an open question for practitioners.

Recurrent neural networks (RNNs), particularly LSTMs, were the state‑of‑the‑art for sequence modelling before transformers. They process text sequentially, maintaining a hidden state that captures context. Transformers, introduced by Vaswani et al. in 2017, process text in parallel using attention mechanisms, capturing long‑range dependencies more effectively. Pre‑trained transformers like BERT have achieved remarkable performance across NLP tasks, but at the cost of increased computational requirements.

This paper provides a controlled comparison of these two approaches on a standard sentiment analysis dataset — the IMDb movie review dataset. We evaluate a bidirectional LSTM, DistilBERT, and BERT base, measuring accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and training time.

2. Methodology

2.1 Dataset

The IMDb movie review dataset contains 50,000 reviews, balanced between positive and negative sentiments (25,000 each). It is split into 25,000 training and 25,000 test samples. The average review length is approximately 200 words, making it a challenging benchmark for sentiment analysis.

2.2 Models

Three models are implemented and evaluated:

  • BiLSTM: A two‑layer bidirectional LSTM with 256 hidden units, followed by dropout (0.5) and a linear classifier. Inputs are tokenised with a 20,000‑word vocabulary and padded to 256 tokens.
  • DistilBERT: A distilled version of BERT with 66 million parameters (vs BERT's 110 million). It is fine‑tuned for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 2e‑5.
  • BERT base: The full BERT base model (uncased, 12 layers, 110 million parameters). It is fine‑tuned for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 2e‑5.

All models are implemented in PyTorch and run on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU (16 GB VRAM).

3.2 Accuracy vs Training Time Trade‑off

While BERT achieves the highest accuracy, it requires 5.2× longer training time than the BiLSTM (94 min vs 18 min). DistilBERT offers a compromise: 91.2 % accuracy (only 0.9 percentage points lower than BERT) with 50 % less training time. For resource‑constrained environments, the BiLSTM remains a practical choice, achieving 87 % accuracy with minimal computational cost.

4. Discussion

The superior performance of BERT stems from its pre‑training on a massive corpus (BooksCorpus + English Wikipedia), which imbues it with rich syntactic and semantic knowledge. Even with only 3 epochs of fine‑tuning, BERT generalises better than the LSTM, which learns embeddings from scratch. The transformer architecture also captures long‑range dependencies more effectively than recurrent networks, which struggle with very long sequences due to vanishing gradients.

Error analysis shows that both models confuse strongly negative reviews with sarcastic positive ones, but BERT captures contextual negation ("not bad at all") more reliably. This is a common challenge for sentiment analysis; pre‑trained transformers have an advantage because they have seen more examples of nuanced language during pre‑training.

The main limitation of this study is the focus on a single dataset. IMDb reviews are relatively formal and well‑structured; performance on noisy, informal text (e.g., social media posts) may differ. Future work should extend this comparison to more diverse datasets.

5. Conclusion

For production sentiment analysis where accuracy is paramount and computational resources are sufficient, BERT is the clear choice. For rapid prototyping or low‑resource environments, a well‑tuned LSTM remains a solid baseline. DistilBERT offers an excellent middle ground, providing near‑BERT performance at half the cost. This benchmark provides a concrete reference for developers choosing between recurrent and transformer architectures on a standard text classification task.
