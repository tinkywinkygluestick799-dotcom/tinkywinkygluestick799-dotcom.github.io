Comparing recurrent and transformer architectures for sentiment analysis: LSTM versus BERT on the IMDb movie review dataset

Alishba Zafar  |  Independent Researcher  |  ← back to portfolio
Manuscript prepared: June 2026  |  Keywords: LSTM, BERT, sentiment analysis, IMDb, NLP benchmarking

Abstract

We compare two fundamentally different neural architectures for binary sentiment classification: a bidirectional LSTM (long short‑term memory) and a fine‑tuned BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) model. Using the balanced IMDb movie review dataset (50,000 reviews), we evaluate accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score. BERT achieves 92.1 % accuracy, outperforming the LSTM by 4.8 percentage points (87.3 %). The advantage is consistent across all metrics, confirming that transformer‑based pre‑training provides a substantial boost even for relatively small‑scale sentiment tasks. The LSTM, however, trains 5× faster and requires significantly less GPU memory, making it a reasonable choice for resource‑constrained environments.

1. Methods

Both models are implemented in PyTorch. The LSTM is a 2‑layer bidirectional network with 256 hidden units, followed by dropout (0.5) and a linear classifier. Inputs are tokenised with a 20k vocabulary and padded to 256 tokens. BERT (bert‑base‑uncased) is fine‑ended for 3 epochs with a learning rate of 2e‑5. The dataset is split 80/20 for training/testing. All experiments are performed on a single NVIDIA T4 GPU (16 GB VRAM).

3. Discussion

The superior performance of BERT stems from its pre‑training on a massive corpus (BooksCorpus + English Wikipedia), which imbues it with rich syntactic and semantic knowledge. Even with only 3 epochs of fine‑tuning, BERT generalises better than the LSTM, which learns embeddings from scratch. The trade‑off is computational cost: BERT requires 5× longer training and ≈4× more memory. DistilBERT offers a reasonable middle ground (91.2 % F1 at half the training time).

Error analysis shows that both models confuse strongly negative reviews with sarcastic positive ones, but BERT captures contextual negation ("not bad at all") more reliably.

4. Conclusion

For production sentiment analysis where accuracy is paramount and computational resources are sufficient, BERT is the clear choice. For rapid prototyping or low‑resource environments, a well‑tuned LSTM remains a solid baseline. This benchmark provides a concrete reference for developers choosing between recurrent and transformer architectures on a standard text classification task.
